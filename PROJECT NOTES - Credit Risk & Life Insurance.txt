 PROJECT 1: West Africa Microfinance Credit Risk Scorecard
 Live at: https://credit-risk-scorecard.vercel.app
 GitHub:  https://github.com/Balisa50/credit-risk-scorecard

================================================================
WHAT IS IT?
================================================================
A credit scoring system. It takes a borrower's information
(age, income, job sector, loan size, past defaults, etc.) and
outputs a score between 300-850. High score = trustworthy
borrower. Low score = high risk of default.

Think of it like a credit score system built specifically for
small loans in West Africa (microfinance).

================================================================
WHAT PROBLEM DOES IT SOLVE?
================================================================
Microfinance banks in West Africa give small loans to people
who often have no formal credit history. Without a scoring
system, the bank either:
  - Rejects everyone (loses business)
  - Approves everyone (loses money to defaults)

This model gives them a data-driven way to decide who to lend
to and at what terms.

================================================================
HOW IT WORKS (in plain language)
================================================================
1. DATA
   - 12,000 synthetic borrowers from West Africa
   - Features: age, gender, income, sector (farming/trade/etc),
     loan amount, debt-to-income ratio, previous defaults,
     group lending status, collateral

2. FEATURE SELECTION (WoE / IV)
   - Weight of Evidence: converts every variable into a score
     that says "how much does this predict default?"
   - Information Value tells you which variables are actually
     useful. IV > 0.3 = strong predictor.
   - Weak variables are dropped before modelling.

3. THE SCORECARD MODEL
   - Standard: Basel II (international banking standard)
   - Algorithm: Logistic Regression converted to a points system
   - Target score: 600 points = 50:1 odds of being good borrower
   - Every 20 points = double the odds (PDO = 20)

4. VALIDATION
   - Gini coefficient: 0.56 (industry threshold is >0.30)
   - KS statistic: 0.42 (measures separation between good/bad)
   - PSI: population stability check (is the model still valid?)
   - ROC curve: shows how well the model picks good vs bad

5. STRESS TESTING
   - "What if a recession hits?" scenarios:
     Baseline, Mild Stress (+30% defaults), Severe (+80%),
     Extreme (+150%)
   - Shows how the portfolio quality degrades under stress

HOW TO USE THE WEBSITE
================================================================
Go to: https://credit-risk-scorecard.vercel.app

Sections on the page:
 - KEY METRICS CARDS (top): Gini, KS, total loans, default rate
 - IV BAR CHART: which features predict default most strongly
 - SCORECARD COEFFICIENTS: the actual model weights
 - ROC CURVE: model discrimination ability

================================================================
HOW ACCURATE IS IT?
================================================================
Gini = 0.56 means the model correctly ranks borrowers 78% of
the time (Gini = 2 x AUC - 1, AUC = 0.78).

Industry benchmark for microfinance credit scoring:
 - Below 0.30 Gini: poor, don't use
 - 0.30-0.45: acceptable
 - 0.45-0.60: GOOD — this model is here
 - Above 0.60: excellent (rare with limited data)

KS = 0.42 is also solid. Banks typically accept KS > 0.30.

IMPORTANT CAVEAT: This uses SYNTHETIC (fake) data generated
to be realistic. In production, you'd train this on REAL loan
history data. The model structure and methodology are correct
and production-ready. The numbers are illustrative, not real
bank data.

 PROJECT 2: Life Insurance Risk Model
 Live at: https://life-insurance-risk.vercel.app
 GitHub:  https://github.com/Balisa50/life-insurance-risk
================================================================

================================================================
WHAT IS IT?
================================================================
An actuarial risk model for life insurance. It:
  1. Models how people die (mortality)
  2. Predicts which policyholders are at highest risk
  3. Calculates how much to charge for life cover (premium)
  4. Simulates how much the insurer might owe in claims (reserve)

Built specifically for Sub-Saharan African mortality patterns.

================================================================
WHAT PROBLEM DOES IT SOLVE?
================================================================
An insurance company selling life cover in Sub-Saharan Africa
needs to answer:
  - How many of my 10,000 policyholders will die this year?
  - Which ones are most at risk?
  - How much should I charge each person?
  - How much money must I keep in reserve for claims?
  - What happens if a pandemic doubles mortality?

This model answers all of those questions.

================================================================
HOW IT WORKS (in plain language)
================================================================

1. MORTALITY MODEL (Gompertz-Makeham)
   - A mathematical formula that models death risk by age
   - A = background risk (accidents, external)
   - B x c^age = the aging component (older = higher risk)
   - Calibrated to Sub-Saharan Africa: includes higher infant
     mortality, higher young-adult mortality (accidents, maternal)
   - Outputs: qx (probability of dying at each age)
   - Life table shows: who survives, life expectancy at each age
   - e(0) = 68.8 years at birth (realistic for the region)

2. 10,000 SYNTHETIC POLICYHOLDERS
   Each person has:
   - Age (20-64), gender, smoker status, BMI, health score (1-5)
   - Sum assured (payout if they die: $10k to $250k)
   - Policy term (5 to 30 years)

3. SURVIVAL ANALYSIS (Kaplan-Meier)
   - Tracks who survives and who dies during their policy term
   - Draws survival curves: "out of 1000 people alive at year 1,
     how many are still alive at year 5, 10, etc.?"
   - Split by: gender, smoker vs non-smoker, health score
   - Death rate over term: 14.7%

4. COX PROPORTIONAL HAZARDS MODEL
   - Answers: "which factors increase your death risk and by how much?"
   - Results (hazard ratios):
       Smoker:        1.7x higher risk of dying
       Male:          1.12x higher risk vs female
       Poor BMI:      up to 1.3x higher (obese/underweight)
       Health score:  0.15x increase per score level (1=excellent, 5=poor)
   - Concordance index: 0.77 (model correctly ranks who dies first
     77% of the time. Random = 0.50, perfect = 1.00)
   - This is strong for mortality data.

5. ACTUARIAL PRICING (Net Single Premium)
   - Formula: NSP = sum of (present value of death benefit x probability of dying)
   - Discount rate: 6% (time value of money)
   - Expense loading: 15% added on top of net premium
   - Results:
       Average net single premium: $3,614
       Average annual gross premium: $373
       Total annual premium income: $3.7 million

6. MONTE CARLO SIMULATION
   - Runs 5,000 scenarios of what could happen over 5 years
   - In each scenario, each policyholder randomly lives or dies
     based on their risk
   - Outputs a distribution of total claims
   - Key results:
       Mean claims (expected): $11.8 million
       VaR 99.5%:              $14.8 million
       (worst case in 1 out of 200 scenarios)
       TVaR 99.5%:             $15.1 million
       (average of the very worst scenarios)
       Required reserve:       $14.8 million
       (how much cash the insurer must hold)

7. STRESS TESTING
   - Pandemic scenarios with mortality shocks:
       Baseline (no shock):   $11.8M mean claims, $14.8M VaR
       Mild pandemic (+25%):  $14.7M mean claims, $18.1M VaR
       Severe (+60%):         $18.7M mean claims, $22.2M VaR
       Catastrophic (+150%):  $28.6M mean claims, $33.0M VaR

================================================================
HOW ACCURATE IS IT?
================================================================

WHAT IS ACCURATE:
 - Concordance index 0.77 is genuinely strong for a Cox PH model
   on simulated mortality data. Real insurance models aim for
   0.70-0.85 range. This hits that target.
 - The Gompertz-Makeham model is the ACTUAL formula used by
   actuaries worldwide. Not invented — it's the industry standard.
 - The premium calculation formula (net single premium) is
   exactly what actuaries use in practice.
 - The Monte Carlo VaR/TVaR methodology is standard in Solvency II
   (European insurance regulation) and similar frameworks.
 - Hazard ratios (smokers 1.7x, males 1.12x) are calibrated to
   match published epidemiological data. They are realistic.

WHAT IS SYNTHETIC (not real data):
 - The 10,000 policyholders are generated, not real people
 - The exact dollar amounts ($11.8M mean claims etc.) are outputs
   of synthetic simulation, not real insurance book data
 - In production, you'd feed this real policyholder data and
   real mortality tables (from government statistics or industry
   experience studies)

BOTTOM LINE:
 - The methodology is correct and production-grade
 - The code is what an entry-level actuary or insurance data
   scientist would be expected to know and build
 - The numbers are realistic (not made up) but from synthetic data
 - If you replaced the synthetic data with real data, the model
   would work for a real insurer
